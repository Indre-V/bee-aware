Bee-Aware is a community that raises awareness to protect diversity and habitats for our pollinators. Every third bite of food that we eat is made possible by pollinators. They are essential not only for pollinating our food crops but also for the survival of other wild plants that provide so much sustenance for our wildlife.

Importance

Critical Ecosystem Service: Pollination is a crucial ecosystem service provided by insects. They facilitate the reproduction of flowering plants by transferring pollen from the male reproductive organs (anthers) to the female reproductive organs (stigma) of flowers, enabling the production of seeds and fruits.

Bee Value: Bees are among the most effective and important pollinators. Honeybees, bumblebees, and solitary bees play vital roles in pollinating a wide variety of crops, including fruits, vegetables, and nuts.

Butterfly and Moth Contributions: Butterflies and moths, known as lepidopterans, also contribute significantly to pollination. They are attracted to brightly colored flowers and are often specialized to pollinate specific plant species.

Global Impact: Approximately 75% of the world's food crops depend, at least in part, on pollination. Pollinators contribute to the reproduction of many fruits, vegetables, nuts, and oilseeds.

Issues

A third of Ireland's 98 wild bee species are in danger of going extinct, contributing to the reduction of pollinators. The issue is grave and needs to be addressed right now to safeguard the sustainability of our food supply to prevent more harm to the agriculture industry's bottom line and preserve the environment.Some wild pollinator species have seen changes in range, abundance, and seasonal behaviours in recent decades due to climate change. Wild pollinator species' natural habitat has been reduced because of human land use changes and landscape alteration. Environmental contamination has deteriorated natural habitats at the same time. Pollinators' access to food and nesting sites is frequently reduced by habitat deterioration, fragmentation, and destruction.

Initiatives

Avoid using insecticides, fungicides and herbicides. These powerful chemical concoctions are intended to destroy a variety of creatures. Refusing to use them is one of the best things you can do for pollinators. Even plants with the label "bee friendly" can have had pesticide treatment. Research has shown that insecticides can kill, injure, and confuse pollinators. Herbicides destroy the "weeds" that are essential to their diet.

Select plants high in nectar and pollen.You can choose from a wide variety of lovely plants that are high in pollen and nectar for your garden. Herbs like rosemary and thyme feed pollinators and you at the same time. Bacopa and bidens make excellent bedding plants for hanging baskets and tiny containers. Don't put these plants in the surrounding environment; instead, confine them to your garden. The best thing you can do for pollinators outside your garden is to mow less and establish biodiversity meadows and borders.

Spread the word. Talk to your council, school, college, business, or spiritual community. Join a community group or Tidy Towns. Spread the word to your friends and family about these ten best practices for protecting pollinators. When news gets out, things change. A single conversation could result in a shift in the way an important environment is managed. We will eventually have a network of environments where pollinators can live and prosper if that person tells one person, and so on.

Establish pollinator nesting areas. Natural pollinators build their nests in the ground, dry stone walls, hedgerows, and untamed places. Simply remove some bare dirt, let certain areas grow wild, or drill holes 10 cm deep in unfinished wood for solitary bees to create a nesting site for them. To live and breed, as well as to support the growth and development of the pollinators of the following year, wild pollinators require secure nesting places for cover from predators and protection from the elements.

Events

No Mow MayPlantlife in the UK launched an annual campaign this year asking people to donate their lawn mowers to local wildlife during the month of May. For our hungry wild pollinators, native Irish wildflowers such as dandelion, clover, and birds-foot trefoil offer the best source of pollen and nectar.

The Wild Bee FestivalTralee Bay Wetlands Eco & Activity Park, Co. Kerry, on the 12th and 13th of August. The National Biodiversity Data Centre is organizing an event with the goal of educating people about Ireland's pollinators and how to support them in their communities.

Polinator WeekInternational celebration honoring the health of pollinators. It's an occasion to honor pollinators and raise awareness of the steps we can take to safeguard them. Pollinator-friendly gardening, garden tours, online workshops for bee and butterfly identification, and much more are popular events.

Protecting Farmland Pollinators Farm WalkObserve ecologically sound land management techniques that support resilient climatic systems, pollinators, and clean water and land. Innovative and useful information on reducing the use of pesticides, creating habitats, improving soil health, pollinator biology, pollinator conservation, and other topics.
